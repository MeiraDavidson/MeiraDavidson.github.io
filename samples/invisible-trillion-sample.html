Sand, Light, and the World's Most Precise Factories

from The Invisible Trillion — Chips, data centers, and the things you can't see

How a Grain of Sand Becomes the Thing in Your Pocket

Pick up a single grain of sand.

Not metaphorically. If you’re somewhere you can get to sand — a beach, a sandbox, a kid’s toy — go get a grain. Put it on your finger. If you can’t get a grain, look at a pinch of salt on your kitchen counter; it’s about the same size. Zoom in on it in your head.

That grain of sand is made mostly of silicon dioxide — silicon bonded to oxygen. To turn it into a chip, you need to pull the silicon out of it, purify the silicon until it is almost unbelievably pure, grow it into a perfect crystal, slice that crystal into wafers, and then — through about 500 to 1,000 separate process steps conducted in the cleanest rooms ever built — draw billions of transistors onto it using light that doesn’t exist anywhere in nature.

The whole process takes about three months. It uses machines that cost $350 million each. It happens, for the most advanced chips, in about five buildings in the world, most of them on one island in the Pacific. And if any one of those 500 steps goes wrong — if a single speck of dust lands in the wrong place, or a single machine is off by three nanometers, or a single drop of the wrong chemical touches the wafer — the chip is ruined.

This chapter is going to walk you through the whole thing, from the grain of sand to the finished chip in your phone. It’s the most interesting chapter in the book, in my opinion, because the process is so physically astonishing that it sounds made up. It is not made up. It is just very strange.

Step 1: Making the Silicon

Regular sand is maybe 50–80% silicon dioxide, mixed with a lot of other stuff. That’s not pure enough. For chips, you need something close to pure silicon.

The first step is to heat silicon-rich sand in a furnace with carbon, which pulls the oxygen away and leaves “metallurgical grade” silicon — about 98% pure. That’s pure enough for solar panels and some industrial uses. For chips, it is laughably impure.

So the metallurgical-grade silicon gets converted into a gas called trichlorosilane, which is then distilled — the same way you would distill vodka, but at much higher stakes — and the distillation is repeated over and over until the silicon is 99.9999999% pure. Nine nines. This is called “9N” purity in the industry. It means there is less than one non-silicon atom per billion silicon atoms.

To give you a sense of what this level of purity means: if you took a swimming pool full of pure water and dropped a single grain of salt in it, the salt would be about a thousand times more contaminant than the impurities in electronic-grade silicon. This is the cleanest substance humans routinely manufacture.

The pure silicon comes out of the distillation process as a powder. Now you need to turn it into a single, enormous, flawless crystal.

Step 2: Growing the Ingot

Here is where the process first starts to feel genuinely magical.

The silicon powder gets melted in a crucible at about 1,400°C. A tiny “seed crystal” of pure silicon — a fingertip-sized piece with the crystal structure already established — is lowered on a rod until it just touches the surface of the molten silicon. Then the rod is very slowly pulled upward, and rotated, while the crucible is also rotated, in the opposite direction.

As the seed crystal rises, silicon atoms from the melt latch onto it one by one, in the orientation dictated by the seed’s crystal structure. A cylinder of perfect, single-crystal silicon grows downward from the seed. This process is called the Czochralski method, after Jan Czochralski, who invented it in 1915 after — the story goes — accidentally dipping his pen into molten tin instead of his inkwell.

The finished cylinder — called an ingot — is about 300 millimeters across (the diameter of a large pizza), up to 2 meters long, and weighs several hundred kilograms. Inside, the atoms are arranged in a single continuous crystal lattice. Not one crystal per region, connected to other crystals at grain boundaries like most metals. One crystal. The entire ingot is, in a real sense, a single molecule. This is maybe the largest perfect thing humans ever make on purpose.

Pulling the ingot takes about two days. If you do it wrong — rotate it too fast, pull it too quickly, let a vibration shake the crucible — the crystal structure gets disrupted and you have to melt it down and start over.

Step 3: Slicing the Wafers

The ingot now gets sliced into thin disks, the way you would slice a salami.

The slicing is done with diamond-coated wires — essentially microscopic cheese wires studded with industrial diamond dust — that cut through the silicon at carefully controlled speeds. Each slice is about 0.7 millimeters thick. A single 2-meter ingot yields a few thousand wafers.

Then the wafers get polished. And then polished some more. And then polished even more. The finished wafer is a mirror so flat that if you expanded it to the size of North America, the tallest “mountain” on its surface would be about the height of a paperback book. The flatness is measured in atoms.

One polished 300mm wafer — the size they use for most modern chips — sells for maybe $200 before any processing. By the time the chips on it are finished, that same wafer is worth somewhere between $15,000 and $20,000, depending on what’s on it. Essentially all of that value is added by what happens next.

Step 4: The Cleanroom

Before we talk about what happens next, we have to talk about where it happens, because the environment is part of the story.

A modern chip fab is built around one big room — or, in the largest fabs, a few big rooms — called the cleanroom. The cleanroom has to be cleaner than anywhere else on Earth, including operating rooms, including labs handling dangerous pathogens, including the clean rooms NASA uses to build spacecraft. The reason is that a chip’s features are so small — a few nanometers — that a single dust particle landing on the wafer at the wrong moment covers thousands of transistors and ruins the chip.

The cleanroom is rated by particle count. A normal room has somewhere between 100,000 and 10 million particles per cubic foot of air (particles larger than 0.5 microns). A hospital operating room might be 10,000. A chip fab’s most sensitive areas — called Class 1 or ISO 3 cleanrooms — have fewer than 12 particles per cubic foot.

Often fewer than 1.

To achieve this, the air in the cleanroom is completely replaced about 600 times per hour, flowing downward from the ceiling through HEPA filters, past the wafers, and out through gratings in the floor. The floor is raised and the ceiling is mostly filters; air comes down from above and is sucked out from below, in a continuous vertical river that keeps particles from getting anywhere near the wafers.

The humans in the cleanroom — and there are humans, though fewer every year — wear full-body “bunny suits” with hoods, face masks, gloves, and booties. The suits are not for the humans’ protection. They are for the chips’ protection. A person is, from the chip’s perspective, a giant walking contamination machine that sheds thousands of skin cells, hair fragments, and fibers per minute. The suit is there to contain all of that.

Humans in the cleanroom move slowly, to avoid disturbing the airflow. They don’t sneeze (or they go immediately to a decontamination area if they do). They can’t wear makeup or perfume. Their movements are tracked, and the entry airlocks involve an elaborate sequence of gowning in a specific order — booties first, then the suit, then the hood, then the mask, then the gloves — that takes about ten minutes to do correctly.

All of this is because, again: a single speck of dust can ruin a chip worth hundreds of dollars, and the chip is one of hundreds on a wafer worth $20,000.

Step 5: The Recipe

Inside the cleanroom, the wafer will go through somewhere between 500 and 1,000 process steps before it becomes a finished chip. That’s not a typo. Five hundred to a thousand.

Each step does one small thing. Deposit a thin layer of material. Etch a pattern into that layer. Implant some atoms. Clean the surface. Inspect. Deposit the next layer. Etch the next pattern. And so on, for months.

The whole sequence is called a “process flow” or “recipe,” and it is the single most valuable piece of intellectual property at a chip company. The recipe for a cutting-edge chip is the collective work of thousands of engineers over years and costs hundreds of millions of dollars to develop. A rival company that stole the recipe could save itself a decade of work.

I am going to describe the three most important kinds of steps, because once you have them, you have the basic building blocks of everything a fab does.

Step 6: Photolithography (The One That Matters)

If you are going to understand only one thing about chip making, make it this.

Almost every step in the recipe depends on patterning — on making a specific, precise shape on the wafer. The transistors are shapes. The wires connecting them are shapes. The insulation between them is shaped. Every layer of the chip is a shape, and the shapes have to line up, layer after layer, to within a few nanometers of accuracy.

The way you make these shapes is called photolithography, and it works like this.

You start with a wafer coated in a thin layer of a chemical called photoresist. Photoresist is goo that changes its chemistry when light hits it. You’ve used something a bit like it if you’ve ever been to a summer camp that made sun-print T-shirts.

You take a mask — a quartz plate with an incredibly precise pattern etched into it in chrome — and you shine a specific kind of light through the mask onto the photoresist-coated wafer. The light passes through the clear parts of the mask and is blocked by the chrome parts. Where the light hits the photoresist, it changes the resist’s chemistry. Then you rinse the wafer with a developer solution, which washes away either the exposed parts or the unexposed parts (depending on which kind of resist you used), leaving behind a precise pattern.

That pattern is now the template for whatever you do next. If you etch the wafer, the etching happens only where the resist is gone. If you deposit metal, the metal sticks to the exposed silicon and sits on top of the resist. And so on.

This process — expose, develop, etch/deposit — gets repeated dozens of times per chip, with different masks for each layer. One modern chip might go through 80 different lithography steps, each with its own mask, each of which has to line up with the ones before it to nanometer precision.

Here is the catch. The smallest feature you can make with photolithography is roughly the wavelength of the light you’re using. Visible light has wavelengths between 400 and 700 nanometers, which means you can’t pattern features smaller than a few hundred nanometers with visible light. The chip industry passed “a few hundred nanometers” in the 1990s, which means for the last 30 years the industry has been desperately trying to use shorter and shorter wavelengths of light to make smaller and smaller features.

Now we need to talk about the hero of this whole book.

Step 7: EUV — The Most Absurd Machine on Earth

The current state of the art is extreme ultraviolet lithography, or EUV. The light used has a wavelength of 13.5 nanometers. That is about 30 times shorter than the shortest visible light. It is, technically, a form of soft X-ray.

Here is how you generate EUV light. You get a tiny drop of molten tin, about 30 micrometers across. You drop it through a vacuum chamber. As it falls, you hit it with a high-power carbon dioxide laser — first a small pulse to flatten it into a pancake, then a much bigger pulse to vaporize it into a plasma hotter than the surface of the Sun. The plasma, briefly, emits EUV light. You catch some of that light with a curved mirror and bounce it through a series of other mirrors — ordinary lenses don’t work for EUV because EUV is absorbed by basically everything, including air — and eventually onto the wafer.

You do this 50,000 times per second. One tin droplet, two laser pulses, one flash of EUV, fifty thousand times a second, for the entire time the machine is running.

The mirrors that carry the EUV light are themselves miracles. EUV is absorbed by glass, by quartz, by almost any material. The only thing that reflects EUV efficiently is a multilayer stack of about 100 alternating layers of molybdenum and silicon, each about two nanometers thick, deposited with atomic precision. Even these mirrors only reflect about 70% of the light that hits them. After bouncing off a dozen mirrors in the machine, only a few percent of the original light reaches the wafer.

The machine that does all of this — called an EUV scanner — is built by exactly one company in the world, a Dutch company called ASML. Each scanner: Contains about 100,000 precisely-engineered parts.

Costs about $350 million.

Weighs 180 metric tons.

Ships from ASML’s factory in the Netherlands to the fab in 40 shipping containers, typically via a dedicated fleet of Boeing 747 cargo planes (because shipping by sea takes too long and doesn’t handle vibration well enough).

Takes several months to install and calibrate.

Is so sensitive that the gravitational pull of the Moon causes small but measurable shifts in its alignment, which have to be corrected for.

ASML has a backlog of orders that stretches years into the future. Every leading-edge chip fab in the world has to buy their EUV machines from this one factory in one Dutch city. If anything happened to ASML, the chip industry as we know it would freeze.

This situation — the entire advanced computing industry depending on a single Dutch company, which in turn depends on a single Belgian company (Zeiss SMT) for the mirrors — is one of the strangest facts about the modern world. Most people don’t know it.

Now you do.

Step 8: Etching, Deposition, and Doping

Lithography just draws the pattern. The other steps actually build the chip.

Deposition is how you add material. Various kinds of deposition machines — chemical vapor deposition, atomic layer deposition, sputtering — add thin films of metal, insulator, or other materials to the wafer, sometimes one atomic layer at a time. A modern chip is made of 50 to 100 of these layers, stacked precisely on top of each other.

Etching is how you remove material. Where the lithography left exposed silicon or metal, etching tools use plasmas or chemicals to dissolve the exposed material away, leaving only the pattern you wanted. Modern etching is so precise it can remove material one atomic layer at a time, without touching the adjacent layer at all. This capability didn’t exist 20 years ago. Now it’s routine.

Doping is how you make the silicon actually behave as a transistor. Pure silicon, interestingly, is a lousy conductor. You make it useful by injecting small amounts of other elements — boron, phosphorus, arsenic — into specific regions of the silicon. These “dopants” change how electrons move through those regions, and different arrangements of dopants create transistors that either block current or let it flow, depending on the voltage applied. The whole idea of a transistor is, at bottom, a choreography of doped silicon regions. Modern fabs do this with ion implantation machines that accelerate the dopant atoms to high speeds and shoot them into the silicon surface like a very, very small gun.

These three operations — deposit a layer, pattern it with lithography, etch it into shape, occasionally dope it — get repeated, in different combinations, for every one of the hundred-ish layers of a modern chip. This is why the process has 500–1,000 steps.

Step 9: Testing, Dicing, Packaging

After about three months in the fab, the wafer is covered in hundreds of chips, each a few square millimeters across. The chips are still all attached to each other on the wafer, and none of them have been tested yet.

Now comes wafer probe — a machine that lands tiny needles on each chip, one at a time, and runs a battery of electrical tests. Chips that pass get marked “good”; chips that fail get marked “bad.” The fraction that pass is called the yield, and it is one of the most important numbers in the industry. A fab running at 90% yield is doing great. A fab at 50% yield is losing a lot of money. When a new process is introduced, yield can start as low as 10–20% and gradually climb as engineers debug the process over months and years.

After probing, the wafer gets diced — cut up into individual chips with a precise saw. Each chip is now a tiny, fragile piece of silicon. These are called die (plural: also die; never “dice” in the industry, for reasons).

Each good die gets packaged: bonded to a substrate, connected to external pins or balls, and encapsulated in the black plastic or ceramic case that’s what you see when you look at a chip in a circuit. Packaging is a whole additional manufacturing process, often done in a different factory in a different country than the fab.

Then the packaged chips get tested again, more thoroughly, and the ones that pass go into boxes and ship to customers.

The whole thing, from silicon powder to boxed chip, takes roughly three to four months. The chip in your phone was being made, somewhere, while you were doing something in the spring, and it reached your phone by Christmas.

Where All This Happens

Here’s a fact that matters more than it should: the number of places in the world that can make the most advanced chips is very small.

TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company), on the island of Taiwan, makes about 90% of the world’s most advanced chips. Their most advanced fabs — the ones making 3-nanometer chips in 2026 — are mostly clustered in the Hsinchu Science Park in northern Taiwan. One island, a couple of industrial parks, a large fraction of the world’s leading-edge computing.

Samsung, in South Korea, is the main competitor to TSMC at the leading edge and makes a large share of the world’s memory chips. Their fabs are in Pyeongtaek and a few other sites in South Korea.

Intel makes its own chips in fabs in Arizona, Oregon, New Mexico, Ireland, and Israel. Intel used to be the world leader in manufacturing technology; they have fallen behind TSMC and Samsung over the last decade and are trying to catch back up.

SMIC (Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation), in China, makes less-advanced chips in large volume, and is slowly pushing into more advanced nodes despite being cut off from buying EUV machines by US export controls.

ASML, in the Netherlands, makes the lithography machines that all of the above depend on.

Zeiss SMT, in Germany, makes the optics for ASML’s machines.

If you drew a map of “places a modern chip’s existence depends on,” it would be a handful of dots: Taiwan, South Korea, the Netherlands, Germany, the US West Coast and Israel. That’s it. Everything else — the software, the chemicals, the inspection equipment — is distributed. But the chokepoints, the places where a fire or an earthquake or a war could take down the whole industry, are in those few dots.

This is why you have probably heard, in the news, a lot of talk about Taiwan, about US chip subsidies (the CHIPS Act in 2022), about export controls on EUV machines to China, about new fabs being built in Arizona and Ohio. The geopolitics of chips is not a weird niche topic. It is one of the central issues of our time, and most people’s picture of it is vague. The concentration of manufacturing in a few places that are, in various ways, fragile — Taiwan’s proximity to China; Korea’s proximity to North Korea; the Netherlands’ dependence on a single company — is a story that is going to keep being in the news for your entire life.

What This Chapter Is For

Here is why I wanted you to know all of this.

When you hear someone talk about “chips” — the chip shortage, AI chips, data center chips, the cost of chips — it is easy to picture a chip as a commodity, something that gets made the way t-shirts or toasters get made. Lots of factories around the world, competing on price, cranking out the product.

It’s not like that. The manufacture of modern chips is almost nothing like the manufacture of almost anything else. It happens in five or six buildings, in four or five countries, with machines built by two or three companies, using a process so delicate that a power outage can ruin a week’s production. It is more like a cathedral than a t-shirt factory. The whole modern world — every phone, every data center, every car with airbags, every hospital monitor, every AI model — sits on top of this strange, concentrated, fragile pyramid of manufacturing.

When people talk about the environmental cost of AI and ignore the fact that a fab uses more water than a city, that is missing something real. But when people talk about the environmental cost of AI and forget that the fab also makes the chips in the medical equipment that kept their grandmother alive, that is also missing something real. Everything is connected to everything. You can’t tell a sensible story about one part of it without at least knowing the other parts exist.

The next chapter is about what the chips in those packages actually do — how a CPU is different from a GPU is different from the chip in an airbag sensor, and why the differences matter. After that, we go to the data center, which is where most of the chips that run AI end up.

But for now, close your eyes for a second and picture it: somewhere on Earth, right now, a tin droplet is falling through a vacuum chamber. A laser is pulsing. A flash of ultraviolet light that doesn’t exist in nature is bouncing off a stack of molybdenum mirrors. It hits a silicon wafer in a room cleaner than surgery. An atom-thin layer of material is patterned by that light. And the pattern — one of the hundred that will be made on that wafer that day — becomes, eventually, the thing you use to text your friends about how your day is going.

It’s a weird world. You are living in it.
