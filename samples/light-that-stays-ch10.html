Mindfulness — The View from the Eagle

from The Light That Stays — A Kid's Guide to Meditation

The past mind cannot be grasped. The present mind cannot be grasped. The future mind cannot be grasped. — from the Diamond Sutra There’s an old, old line — more than fifteen hundred years old — that sounds like a riddle. The past mind can’t be grabbed. The present mind can’t be grabbed. The future mind can’t be grabbed. What is that supposed to mean? Stay with it for a minute, because it has something important to say about a word you’ve probably heard a hundred times by now: mindfulness.

It’s everywhere. Mindfulness apps on your phone. Mindfulness classes at school. Companies sending their employees to mindfulness workshops. Famous people swearing by it — Jerry Seinfeld has talked for decades about how Transcendental Meditation changed his life; Ray Dalio, one of the most successful investors alive, says it’s the single most valuable thing he’s ever learned. A whole movement has formed around a practice that used to belong to a few quiet people in caves.

In many ways that’s wonderful. But it’s also meant the word has gotten flattened. Most people now think mindfulness means try harder to focus, or force your mind to stay in the present. One more thing on your to-do list. One more thing you’re failing at. If that’s what mindfulness sounded like to you, no wonder you weren’t excited about it.

Real mindfulness is the opposite of that. Real mindfulness is lighter, not heavier.

The Eagle and the Hallway

Here’s a picture that gets at it. Imagine it’s the worst kind of Tuesday at school. You’re walking through the crowded hallway between classes, and there’s something bad sitting on top of your whole day — somebody said something cruel, your best friend was strange with you at lunch, you bombed a quiz, the group chat went silent the second you sent your message. From the hallway it feels enormous. It fills your entire life. You can’t see past it. Every face streaming by seems to know.

Now imagine an eagle lifts off from beside you and rises straight up — through the ceiling, through the roof, up above the school — until from way up there it can see the whole town. Your school is one small building in a wide, green landscape. Your hallway is one corridor inside that small building. The thing that was crushing you is still down there, but now it’s one tiny piece of one small building in a wide, beautiful world. From up here you can also see the road home, your street, the trees in your yard, the river out past the highway, the sun that will rise over all of it tomorrow.

Mindfulness is what happens when you become, for a moment, the eagle. You don’t leave your life. You just stop being pinned to one square inch of it. You see your situation from a wider sky. From up there, things stop being life-or-death. You stop clinging to your one fixed point of view, your one story about what’s happening. You can see other angles. You can guess how you’ll feel about this in a week, or a year, or five years. The crisis becomes part of a much larger picture.

That is what the Diamond Sutra meant. The past, present, and future “mind” can’t be grabbed because there’s nothing solid there to grab. The moment you reach out to seize now, it’s already gone. The past is gone. The future hasn’t shown up. There’s nothing to clutch. And — surprising no one more than yourself — once you stop trying to clutch, the whole thing gets lighter.

When the Mind Gets Stuck

Here’s something worth knowing about how the mind works, because once you see it you can never quite unsee it. A lot of the worst suffering in life doesn’t come from the original bad thing — it comes from the way the mind keeps coming back to the bad thing. Someone says something cruel at school. You walk away. The cruel thing is over, factually. But your mind plays it again. And again. And every replay makes the groove a little deeper and the feeling a little bigger, until you’ve spun ten bad seconds into a whole bad week.

Everyone does this. Watch the adults around you — you’ll see it everywhere, sometimes painfully so. The mind has a strange habit of latching onto a problem and looping it like a stuck song, and the more it loops, the more real and huge and permanent the problem feels. The loop itself becomes the trap. The mind, in trying to solve the thing, ends up reinforcing the thing — a vicious circle that keeps tightening on the very person caught in it. People can spend years inside a loop like that without ever quite realizing the loop is what’s keeping them there, not the original hurt.

This is exactly where the eagle helps.

When the eagle lifts off, you stop being the one inside the loop and become, for a moment, the one watching it. The hurt is still down there, but you’re not living inside it — you’re observing it from above, the way you’d watch a good friend going through something hard. And something quiet happens when you watch from outside: most of the emotional fog burns off. You can think clearly again. You can see angles you couldn’t see while you were tangled up inside. You can ask the question that the loop never lets you ask: is this actually as huge as it feels right now, or am I making it huger every time I run through it? Sometimes you notice — with a little surprise — that the problem looks different from up here than it did down there.

The eagle doesn’t make the hard thing disappear. What it does is loosen the loop. And the loop, almost always, is doing far more damage than the hard thing itself.

Three Words to Remember

The eagle has three qualities, and these three are what mindfulness actually feels like when it’s real: Curious. Not panicked, not bored. Just genuinely interested in what’s happening, the way the eagle is interested in the landscape below. Hmm — what’s actually here right now?

Open. No walls. You’re willing to see things you didn’t expect, including the parts of yourself you’d rather not look at.

Kind. The eagle doesn’t judge the city. It just watches. You let yourself, and everyone else, be human.

Curious. Open. Kind. If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember those three.

And underneath all three, the deepest quality: the eagle just watches. It doesn’t grasp. It doesn’t try to hold on to the good views or push away the bad ones. It doesn’t judge the landscape — calling this part pretty and that part ugly, then trying to keep one and get rid of the other. It just sees what’s there. Good day, bad day, beautiful, ugly — each one passes through its field of vision and its fist never closes. That’s the part almost everyone misses when they hear the word “mindfulness”: it isn’t about capturing the present moment. It’s the opposite. It’s the willingness to let each moment be exactly what it is — without fixing it, labeling it, keeping it, or pushing it away.

There’s an old word from this whole tradition for that kind of seeing: paramita, which means something like gone beyond, or transcendent. The “beyond” is, mostly, beyond grasping. When mindfulness goes deep, it becomes a paramita — it lifts you beyond the small, anxious, gripping self into a wider sky where you can watch your life without having to clutch at it. That’s what people are really reaching for when they get serious about this, even when they don’t know the word for it.

Walking Meditation

Here is my favorite way to take mindfulness off the cushion and into your day. It’s called walking meditation, and it is exactly what it sounds like: meditating while you walk. Slowly.

Find a path. A quiet hallway. A backyard. A short stretch of sidewalk. Even ten feet of bedroom will do. Stand still for a moment. Feel your feet on the ground, and notice your breath, coming in and going out. Then start to walk, much slower than your normal pace.

As you walk, let your attention rest on all of it at once — your breath, the soles of your feet, the swing of your arms, the air on your skin. If matching your steps to your breath feels natural — a few steps on the in-breath, a few on the out — go with that. Don’t strain. Just feel what’s happening, in detail: the lifting of one foot, the slow swing through the air, the placing back down on the ground, the shift of weight to that foot, and now the other foot lifting. The more closely you feel the details, the quieter your mind goes.

When your mind wanders — and it will, immediately — gently bring it back to the feeling of the steps, or the breath, or whichever sensation is most vivid right then. You’re not trying to think anything in particular. You’re just being the eagle, watching one foot in front of the other.

Try five minutes a day. It will change how you walk through every other part of your life.

And here’s the part that turns walking meditation into something bigger than a five-minute exercise. Once you’ve practiced it formally a few times, you start to remember it anywhere. Walking home from school, walking the dog, walking from your kitchen to your bedroom — you’ll catch yourself midstep, slow your pace a beat, and feel your feet again. The whole world becomes your meditation hall the minute you remember it can be. Some of the clearest moments of my life have come on ordinary walks around the neighborhood, when I suddenly remembered to actually be there — feeling the breeze, hearing a bird I’d been walking right past for years, noticing the light through the leaves. You don’t have to slow down dramatically. You just have to wake up to the walk you were already taking.

Mindfulness Is Not One More Thing

Hear this clearly, because it matters and almost no app will tell you: mindfulness is not one more thing you have to add to your day. It is, exactly, the opposite. It is putting things down.

The world will keep telling you mindfulness is about working harder on yourself, getting more disciplined, never losing focus. That’s wrong. Mindfulness is about unclenching. It’s the eagle taking off — not the eagle dragging more stones up into the air with it.

So start small. The one-minute reset from Chapter 7. The body scan from Chapter 5. Five minutes of walking. As one becomes natural, you’ll want to add another — not because anyone is making you, but because you’ve noticed how much better the day is when you do. You add practices the way you’d add salt to food: a little at a time, until it tastes right. Never as a punishment.

Always as a relief.
