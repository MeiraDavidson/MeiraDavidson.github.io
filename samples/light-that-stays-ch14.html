Kindness Without Keeping Score

from The Light That Stays — A Kid's Guide to Meditation

Stop for a second and think about how you got through today.

The breakfast you ate was grown by farm workers you’ll

never meet — people who were up before dawn, working long hours in heat and cold for wages far too small. It was carried to you by drivers and delivery workers who sometimes can’t even stop for a bathroom break. When you flip a switch, someone you’ll never see is keeping the power on. And if you ever wake up sick and frightened in the middle of the night, there are nurses and doctors awake on that exact night shift, so that a stranger they have never met — you — can be okay.

None of them know your name. Most of them you will never lay eyes on. And still, your whole life — the food, the warmth, the safety, the hundred small comforts you barely notice — is being held up, every single hour, by thousands of quiet hands.

I want you to really feel that, especially on a day when it seems like nobody in the world cares whether you exist. Because here is something true even on that day: you are already being cared for, constantly, by more people than you could ever count. You are not as alone as the feeling tells you. You are woven into an enormous web of people quietly keeping each other alive — almost none of them ever thanked.

Once you really see that web, something in you wants to add your own thread to it. Not out of guilt — guilt is a miserable reason to do anything — but because it simply feels right. You’ve been carried; you find you want to help carry. That’s just the natural shape of a heart that’s paying attention.

And remember what we found earlier in this book: underneath everything, the same awake, aware light is in every person — in you, and in everyone. The farmer and the nurse and the delivery driver — but also the classmate who was cruel to you, and the grown-up who let you down — the same light is in all of them, underneath. Some people help the world in ways you can see, and some in ways you’ll never know about. The person who quietly raises a child, or sweeps a floor, or simply gets through a brutal day without passing their pain on to anyone else, matters every bit as much as the famous face on a screen. There is no such thing as a small life. Every single one is precious. Every single one deserves to be protected. Including yours.

Now here’s the part most people get wrong. They think kindness means giving and giving and then waiting — keeping a quiet scoreboard of who owes them back. That isn’t kindness; it’s a trade. And when the other person doesn’t pay up the way you hoped, it breaks your heart every time.

Real kindness doesn’t keep score.

It means you offer your care freely, and then you let go of what happens next. You don’t grip it. You’re kind because that’s who you have decided to be — not because of what you’ll get in return. The old teachers called this kindness without attachment, and it is one of the most freeing things a person can ever learn. When you stop needing people to respond a certain way, they lose the power to crush you by responding the wrong way. You stay soft. You’re simply no longer breakable.

But — and this matters as much as anything in this whole book — kindness without keeping score has to include being kind to yourself, and you start there. You cannot pour from an empty cup. If you are running on empty, hurting, worn down to nothing, then the wisest and most loving thing you can do is turn some of that care inward first. Rest. Guard your own heart. And know this clearly: being kind to everyone does not mean letting anyone treat you badly. Sometimes the most compassionate thing you can do — for them and for you — is to take a step back, set down someone who keeps hurting you even if you still love them, and keep yourself safe. That is not unkindness. That is wisdom.

And now the hardest part of all.

Sometimes the people who are supposed to love you can’t seem to manage it. A parent who won’t show up. A friend who goes cold. People who turn away from you — maybe even for something about yourself that you finally found the courage to be honest about. When that happens, it doesn’t feel like a disappointment; it feels like a verdict. Something must be wrong with me.

Please read this next sentence twice, because it’s the most important one in the chapter: when someone can’t love you the way you deserve, that is news about where they are — not about whether you are lovable.

People can only give from where they actually stand. Someone trapped in their own fear, their own anger, their own unhealed hurt, will sometimes push away the very people they love the most — not because those people aren’t worth loving, but because the one doing the pushing is too lost, right then, to do any better. It’s their cloud. It was never your light going out. Remember the sky behind the clouds — the sun doesn’t dim just because a cloud rolls in front of it.

Grown-ups are not finished, perfect people. They make mistakes, sometimes enormous ones, sometimes the kind that wound their own kids. You’re allowed to be angry about that. Your hurt is real and you never have to pretend it away. And — when you are ready, not a moment before — you can leave a quiet door open in your heart: maybe, given enough time, they’ll find their way back, because people really do change, slowly, once they finally turn and face what they’ve been running from.

But hold on to this boundary, because it’s the part that protects you: it is not your job to fix them, and it is not your job to wait in pain for them. You can wish them well from a safe distance. You can keep the door open without standing in the cold. And in the meantime, you turn toward the people who can show up for you right now — and there are some, even if they aren’t the ones you most wish it could be. Let the people who are able to love you, love you.

One last thing, so you don’t take this whole chapter and turn it into a heavy backpack you have to carry. You do not have to fix the world, or all of your broken relationships, today. That isn’t how anything real works. The world’s troubles are old and tangled; even the greatest helpers only ever move things forward a little at a time. People who try to fix everything at once tend to burn themselves out, or knock over things that were quietly holding other people up. Wanting good things doesn’t automatically make you wise about how to get them — patience does. So go at a real pace. Protect yourself first. Do the kindness that’s actually in front of you: a kind word, a hand to someone having a worse day than yours, simply not passing your pain down the line to the next person. That is not “too small.” That is exactly how the web grows stronger — one quiet thread at a time.

And the same goes for your own life. When you find something that genuinely interests you and doesn’t hurt anyone, just start. You don’t have to be sure it’s the One True Calling — almost nobody is. Meaning grows up through the doing, the way a plant grows up through soil; you don’t reason a tomato into existence, you plant a seed. Don’t argue yourself out of your own life. Pick something small that feels true, and begin.

And if the sadness ever grows too heavy — heavier than you can carry alone, the kind that just won’t lift — please remember that letting a caring grown-up help you carry it is itself an act of kindness toward yourself. You are not a burden. You are one of those precious, irreplaceable lives that deserve to be protected. You are worth helping, and asking for that help is one of the bravest, kindest things you will ever do.

You are held by a thousand hands you will never see. Add your one thread when you can. Protect your own light first. And be patient — with the world, with the people who couldn’t, and most of all with yourself.
