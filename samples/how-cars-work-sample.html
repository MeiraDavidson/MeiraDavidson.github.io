Suck, Squeeze, Bang, Blow

from How Cars Work — For curious passengers

A car is heavy. Heavier than you, heavier than your mom, heavier than your mom and you and a full week of groceries combined. A normal car weighs about three thousand pounds. That is roughly the same as a small horse, except a horse can move itself and a car cannot, not on its own.

Something has to push it.

When you ride a bicycle, you know what is pushing it: you are. Your legs push the pedals, the pedals turn the chain, the chain turns the back wheel, and the bike rolls forward. If you stop pushing, the bike slows down and stops. The connection between your legs and the wheels is direct. You can feel it.

A car has no pedals you push. (It has pedals your mom pushes with her foot, but those don't push the wheels — they just tell the car what to do. We will get to those later.) So what is pushing a car?

The answer is small explosions. Hundreds of them, every second. Every car like the one your grandmother drove, and most cars on the road today, runs on a controlled, repeating series of tiny explosions inside the engine. The explosions push metal pieces. The metal pieces turn other metal pieces. Eventually the wheels turn, and the car moves.

That is the whole story. The rest of this chapter is the details.

Fire needs three things

To have an explosion, you need fire. To have fire, you need three things: something to burn, something to burn it with, and something to start it.

Something to burn is gasoline. Gasoline is a liquid made from oil that comes out of the ground, and it burns very, very well. If you have ever stood near a pump at a gas station, you have smelled it.

Something to burn it with is air. Specifically, the oxygen in air. About one-fifth of the air around you is oxygen, and oxygen is what makes things burn. Without oxygen, fire cannot exist.

Something to start it is a spark. Gasoline and air sitting together don't do anything. Mix them in a jar and ignore them and they will just sit there. You have to give them a kick. A small electric spark is enough.

Fire needs three things: fuel, air, and a spark.

TRY IT: THE CANDLE AND THE JAR

You will need: A small candle (a tea light works perfectly) A glass jar that is bigger than the candle (a clean pasta-sauce jar is great) A grown-up to help with the lighting Put the candle on a plate. Light it with the grown-up's help and let it burn for a few seconds. Then turn the jar upside down and lower it over the candle, so the mouth of the jar sits flat on the plate and the candle is sealed inside.

Watch what happens. The flame keeps burning for a few seconds. Then it gets smaller. Then it gets very small. Then it goes out. There is still candle left. There is nothing wrong with the wick. The candle did not run out of fuel.

What ran out is the oxygen. Once the flame burned through all the oxygen sealed inside the jar, the fire could not continue. Take the jar off, light the candle again, and it burns happily, because now the air can get to it.

This is exactly what is happening inside a car engine, except instead of a candle you have gasoline, and instead of an open flame you have a controlled explosion. No oxygen, no boom. The whole engine is built around making sure the right amount of fuel meets the right amount of air at exactly the right time.

A metal cup in a metal tube

Now we need something for the explosion to push.

Picture a metal cup that fits snugly inside a metal tube — close enough that nothing leaks past the edges, but loose enough that the cup can slide up and down freely. The cup is called a piston.

The tube is called a cylinder.

If you light a small amount of gasoline-and-air mixture in the closed top of the cylinder, the explosion shoves the piston down.

Hard. Faster than you can blink.

That is the only thing the explosion has to do. Push the piston down. Everything else in the engine is just a clever way to set up the next explosion and turn that downward push into something useful.

A single cylinder during the BANG stroke.

The four strokes

You can't have one explosion and call it done. Cars need to keep going for hours. So the engine has to repeat this same trick — explosion, push, explosion, push — over and over.

To do that, every cylinder repeats the same four-step cycle, called the four-stroke cycle. Each stroke is one movement of the piston, either up or down. The four strokes have nicknames, and the nicknames are accurate and a little funny: 1. SUCK. The piston moves down. A little door at the top of the cylinder (called an intake valve) opens, and the falling piston pulls in a fresh mixture of air and gasoline. Like pulling on the plunger of a syringe.

2. SQUEEZE. The little door slams shut. The piston moves back up, squishing the air-and-gasoline mixture into a tiny space at the top. Squeezing a gas makes it hot and ready to burn.

3. BANG. A spark plug — a small device that can shoot an electric spark on demand — fires. The squished mixture explodes. The piston gets shoved down hard. This is the only stroke where the engine is making power. The other three are just setting it up.

4. BLOW. The piston comes back up. A different little door (the exhaust valve) opens, and the piston pushes the burned, used-up gas out of the cylinder. Then that door slams shut, the intake door opens again, and we are back at SUCK.

Down, up, down, up. Suck, squeeze, bang, blow. Two full trips up and back down for the piston, and the engine has produced one push.

A typical car engine does this somewhere between ten and a hundred times every second, in each cylinder. When you hear a car running, what you are hearing is hundreds of these little cycles per second, blurred into one steady sound.

DID YOU KNOW? The four-stroke cycle was invented by a German engineer named Nicolaus Otto in 1876. Engineers still call it the Otto cycle in his honor, almost 150 years later. Otto wasn't even a trained engineer — he started out as a grocer who got obsessed with engines in his spare time and eventually built one that worked better than anyone else's. Almost every gasoline car ever made uses a version of his idea.

Four cylinders, taking turns

A regular car doesn't have one cylinder. It has four, or six, or eight, all running this same dance, but timed so they take turns. While cylinder 1 is on BANG, cylinder 2 might be on SQUEEZE, cylinder 3 on BLOW, and cylinder 4 on SUCK. They are staggered so that there is always at least one cylinder making power at any given moment.

Think of a Lego model where four little arms all push down on the same gear, but never at the same time. Each arm does its job in turn, and the gear keeps spinning smoothly. That is an engine.

All the pistons push down on the same long metal rod called a crankshaft. The crankshaft has the shape of a few zigzags — bent in the right places so that when a piston pushes straight down, the crankshaft has to turn sideways to make room. Up-and-down motion at the top becomes round-and-round motion at the bottom.

Just like your legs on a bicycle. Your knees go up and down, but the pedals turn in circles. The connecting bar between your knee and the pedal makes the conversion. The crankshaft does the same thing in a car, except with metal arms and explosions instead of your legs and a peanut butter sandwich.

Four pistons and a crankshaft, taking turns.

That's the heartbeat

When you hear a car start, what you are hearing is the four-stroke cycle beginning. A small motor (called the starter) turns the engine over a few times to get the cycle going, and then the explosions take over and the engine runs itself, banging along at thousands of cycles a minute.

When the car is sitting at a red light and you can hear it idling — that low, even rumble under the hood — that is the four-stroke running slowly, just enough to keep itself going. When your mom presses the gas pedal, more fuel and more air flow in, the bangs come faster and harder, and the wheels turn faster.

The whole rest of an old-fashioned car is built around this single trick: the heartbeat in the middle, where small, controlled explosions happen thousands of times a minute, and somehow — except it isn't somehow, it is the next four chapters — that heartbeat ends up turning the wheels under your seat.
