A Memex and a Satellite

from How the World Got Wired — A Brief History of the Internet

In the summer of 1945, an american scientist named Vannevar Bush sat down to write an essay.

The Second World War had just ended. Bush had spent

the previous five years running the most important science office in the United States, called the Office of Scientific Research and Development. He had been the man who told President Franklin Roosevelt, in a famous meeting in 1940, that America needed to start a serious atomic research program — and when Roosevelt agreed, Bush helped make it happen. The bomb that ended the war was, in a sense, his bomb. Or at least, he had helped pay for it.

Now the war was over. Bush was tired. And he was wor‐

ried.

He was worried about a particular problem that most

people did not think was a problem at all. The problem was that science was producing too much information.

Every year, in laboratories around the world, scientists wrote tens of thousands of papers. Each paper described a small new discovery — a new chemical, a new equation, a new way of doing something. The papers were published in journals. The journals piled up in libraries. There were so many papers, in so many journals, in so many libraries, that no human being could possibly read them all. A chemist in Boston might spend years working on a problem that a chemist in Berlin had already solved, and never know it, because the Berlin chemist’s paper was sitting on a shelf in a library nobody had visited.

Bush thought this was a tragedy. So in July of 1945, he wrote an essay called As We May Think, in a magazine called The Atlantic Monthly, and in it he described a machine.

He called the machine the memex.

the memex Vannevar Bush’s imagined memex (1945) — a desk that could link any document to any other.

The memex would be the size of a desk. You would sit at it. Inside the desk, on tiny strips of film, would be stored every book, every article, every photograph, every letter you had ever wanted to keep. You would press a button, and the machine would find you any document in a second. That was useful — but it was not the magical part.

The magical part was that you could connect documents to each other.

If you were reading an article about birds, and the article reminded you of a poem you had read years before, you could press a button and link the article to the poem. From then on, anyone using your memex could jump from the article to the poem instantly. Then, if the poem reminded you of a painting in another book, you could link the poem to the painting. Soon you would build up trails — paths of connected ideas, running through everything you had ever read.

You could share your trails with other people. A scientist could share her trail through the chemistry of the human body. A historian could share his trail through a century of letters between two presidents. A child could share her trail through the books that had made her cry.

Knowledge, Bush wrote, would stop being a pile of separate things. It would become a web.

Vannevar Bush never built the memex. The technology of

1945 was not nearly good enough. He was describing a machine that wouldn’t really be possible for another fifty years. But he had drawn a picture of it, in words, in a magazine that a lot of curious young people read. They remembered it.

They grew up. Some of them became scientists. And when

the time came to build something that actually worked the way Bush had imagined, they remembered the picture.

One of the young people who read Bush’s essay was a

man named J.C.R. Licklider.

You’ll meet him in the next chapter.

But before we get to Licklider, we have to talk about a beach ball.

the Beach Ball Sputnik, the polished metal sphere whose beep panicked an entire country.

On the night of October 4, 1957, a small metal sphere — polished to a mirror finish, about the size of a beach ball, weighing about 184 pounds — separated from the top of a rocket high above the Earth. It had four whip-like radio antennas trailing behind it. It was now in orbit, which meant it was going to circle the planet, over and over, until something pulled it down. On a clear evening, in the right kind of twilight, you could actually look up and see it — a tiny moving star, much faster than any real star should move.

It was called Sputnik. The word means fellow traveler in Russian.

The Soviet Union had launched it.

Inside Sputnik was a simple radio transmitter, designed to send out a beep on a frequency that almost any shortwave radio could pick up. Beep. Beep. Beep. Every fraction of a second, for three weeks, until the batteries ran out.

Sputnik’s signal was on a frequency that almost any shortwave radio could pick up. All over the world — kids with little radios, men in basements, scientists in observatories — people turned their dials, found the right frequency, and listened to the Russian beach ball announcing itself as it passed over their countries every 96 minutes.

In America, this was a catastrophe.

Not because Sputnik itself was dangerous. Sputnik was

just a beeping ball. It couldn’t do anything except beep. But the rocket that had put Sputnik into orbit was the same kind of rocket that could carry a nuclear bomb. The Soviet Union had just demonstrated, in front of the whole planet, that they could launch a heavy object into space and send it precisely to where they wanted.

If they could do that to a beach ball, they could do it to a city.

Worse — much worse, in the minds of many Americans

— the Soviets had done it first. The United States had been working on its own satellite. It wasn’t ready. The Russians had beaten them. And the Russians were the people America was most afraid of, because the world after the Second World War had divided itself into two camps that did not trust each other at all, and the Russians ran the other one.

The American newspapers went wild. Politicians gave panicked speeches. Schools were criticized for not teaching enough math and science. Parents wondered whether the country was raising the wrong kind of children. President Eisenhower, who was usually a calm man, tried to reassure everyone, and mostly failed.

Less than four months later, in February 1958, the United States government created a new agency.

arpa The new agency was called the Advanced Research Projects Agency. People called it ARPA.

ARPA’s job was strange. Most government agencies have a clear thing they do. The Post Office delivers mail. The Department of Agriculture handles farms. ARPA’s job was almost the opposite. ARPA’s job was to handle whatever the regular system couldn’t. It was supposed to fund science projects that were too weird, too long-term, too risky, or too unproven for any normal organization to pay for. Its purpose, in a sentence, was to make sure America was never surprised by another Sputnik.

It had a lot of money. It had very few rules. And — this is the important part — it was allowed to give that money to almost anyone.

Universities. Private companies. Individual scientists with strange ideas. ARPA didn’t need to know exactly what the research would produce. It just needed to believe that the people doing the research were smart, and that what they were trying to do, if it worked, would matter.

This was a new kind of government. For most of Americ‐

an history, when the government wanted scientific work done, it built a laboratory and hired scientists to work in it.

ARPA didn’t build laboratories. ARPA wrote checks.

A lot of the checks went to projects that failed completely. ARPA paid for nuclear-powered airplanes that never flew. For psychic experiments that produced nothing. For weapons that didn’t work. That was fine. The whole point of ARPA was that most of its bets would lose, as long as a few of them won enormously.

A few of them did win. Enormously.

One of the bets ARPA made, in the early 1960s, was on a man at MIT who had read Vannevar Bush’s essay about the memex when he was young, and never quite gotten over it. His name was J.C.R. Licklider, and he had recently written a paper called Man-Computer Symbiosis, in which he argued that computers and people would one day work together so closely that they would think as a single mind.

In October of 1962, ARPA hired Licklider to run a brandnew office, called the Information Processing Techniques Office. He was given a budget. He was told he could spend it more or less however he liked, as long as it was on computers.

He had some ideas about what to do.

He started writing memos to a small list of scientists around the country, addressing them — half-jokingly, halfseriously — as Members and Affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network.

That is where the internet begins.

But Licklider’s story is the next chapter.
