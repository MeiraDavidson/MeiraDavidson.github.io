The Hormone Nobody Was Looking For

from The Hunger Switch — A Drug from a Lizard

Set John Eng aside for a chapter. He'll come back. To understand what he is about to find — what is in the fluid he is collecting from his lizard, why it matters, and why it matters in particular for diabetes — we need to go somewhere else first, and somewhen else: Boston, the early 1980s, a different laboratory, a different problem.

The problem was old. You can date it, more or less, to the spring of 1902, when two English physiologists named William Bayliss and Ernest Starling introduced hydrochloric acid into the small intestine of an anaesthetized dog. Within minutes, the dog's pancreas began secreting digestive juice. This was, on the face of it, surprising. The pancreas was supposed to be controlled by nerves. Bayliss and Starling had cut the relevant nerves. Yet the pancreas had still received a message — and the message, they were able to show, traveled through the blood. Some chemical released by the gut, in the presence of acid, was telling the pancreas what to do.

Bayliss and Starling called the chemical secretin and called the entire class of things like it hormones, from the Greek hormao, "to set in motion." This is, more or less, the founding moment of endocrinology. The intuition behind it is that the body has, alongside its electrical communication system — nerves, signals, twitches — a chemical communication system: messengers traveling through the blood, telling distant tissues what to do, and when, and how much. Hormones are how the gut talks to the pancreas, how the brain talks to the ovaries, how the kidney talks to the bone marrow. They are slow compared to nerves, but they reach everywhere.

A hormone, in chemical terms, can be many things. Some are small molecules — adrenaline, thyroid hormone, the steroids including testosterone and estrogen and cortisol. Others, including all the ones in this book, are peptides: short chains of amino acids, the same amino acids that make up larger proteins. A peptide hormone is, for our purposes, a small protein. Insulin is a peptide hormone. So is glucagon. So, it will turn out, are quite a lot of the messages the gut sends.

In the decades after Bayliss and Starling, physiologists identified more and more of these gut hormones. Each one had its own discovery, its own story, its own moderately confusing name. By the middle of the twentieth century, a particular oddity had attached itself to the gut–pancreas axis, an oddity that resisted easy explanation. Researchers had begun to notice that if you fed someone a meal of glucose by mouth, the resulting rise in insulin was much larger than if you put the same amount of glucose directly into their veins, achieving the same blood sugar level. The mouth route was somehow more powerful than the vein route. Something about eating the sugar — about it passing through the stomach and the small intestine on its way to the bloodstream — primed the pancreas to release more insulin than the sugar itself, by the time it arrived in the blood, would have warranted.

The phenomenon got a name in the 1960s: the incretin effect. The implied chemical responsible — the chemical the gut was using to message the pancreas as food arrived — got called an incretin. Researchers thought there was probably one of these. Maybe two. Identifying them, and characterizing them, became one of the steady minor problems of midtwentieth-century endocrinology.

By 1980, one incretin had been pretty firmly identified: a peptide hormone called GIP, originally for "gastric inhibitory polypeptide," later renamed "glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide" (the field reserves the right to fix its own bad names, provided the new name has the same initials). GIP came from cells in the upper small intestine. It was released when food arrived. It encouraged insulin secretion. It explained, in laboratory experiments, some of the incretin effect.

But not all of it.

If you blocked GIP entirely — which researchers could do, with various clever tricks — the incretin effect did not disappear. It got smaller. Something else was still pushing the pancreas. A second incretin, somewhere in the gut, doing some of the work GIP was thought to do alone. Most people in the field assumed it would turn up. Most people in the field were not particularly excited about looking for it. Gut hormone discovery was unglamorous compared to, say, cancer biology, and the funding agencies in the early 1980s reflected this.

Joel Habener, at Massachusetts General Hospital, was working on something nearby but not quite the same.

Habener was an endocrinologist by training and a molecular biologist by avocation, which in the early 1980s was a useful combination to be. He had come to MGH in the mid-1970s, set up a laboratory in the hospital's research building, and spent the years since chipping away at a single hormone: glucagon.

Glucagon is insulin's opposite number. Insulin tells the body to take sugar out of the blood and store it; glucagon, released by a different population of cells in the same pancreas, tells the body to release sugar back into the blood. Insulin handles the after-meal flood. Glucagon handles the in-between fast. The two together, in healthy people, keep blood sugar within a range narrow enough that the brain — which runs on glucose and dies if deprived of it for more than a few minutes — never notices.

By 1980 a great deal was known about insulin. A great deal less was known about glucagon. The hormone had been purified, sequenced — meaning the order of its amino acids had been worked out — and produced in moderate quantities. But how it was made — how the body went from a stretch of DNA to the finished hormone — was an open question. Habener, with the new tools of recombinant DNA technology that had become available in the late 1970s, was set on answering it.

The thing to understand about a peptide hormone, for our purposes, is that it is not made directly. The DNA in the relevant cells encodes not the finished hormone but a longer precursor protein — sometimes much longer — that gets cut and trimmed, after it is made, into the active hormone. Insulin, for example, is built first as preproinsulin, then trimmed to proinsulin, then cleaved into insulin and a leftover bit called C-peptide. The mature hormone is the small piece you get at the end of an industriallooking series of cuts.

This is a strange way to make things, and biology does it for reasons not fully understood, having to do with folding and storage and quality control. The detail that matters, here, is the cuts. The cuts can produce more than one finished molecule. A long precursor protein, in principle, can be processed into several active hormones, with different functions, depending on where the cellular machinery decides to slice. This was suspected, in the early 1980s, but few cases had been worked out.

Habener's group set out to clone the gene for glucagon. Cloning a gene, in 1980, meant identifying the stretch of DNA that encoded the protein, isolating it, copying it, and reading its sequence — a procedure that took years, in the early days, and ate postdocs at a steady rate. By 1982, working with collaborators in California, Habener's lab had it. They had the sequence of the proglucagon gene — proglucagon being the long precursor that, in pancreatic cells, gets cut into glucagon.

The sequence was longer than they expected.

The gene encoded a precursor protein of one hundred and sixty amino acids. Glucagon is twenty-nine. That left more than a hundred amino acids of precursor doing something else. Most of it was flanking sequence — the sort of biological packing material that surrounds peptide hormones and gets discarded during processing. But within the precursor, alongside glucagon, were two other stretches that looked, structurally, like peptide hormones in their own right. They had the right shape. They had the right kinds of amino acids in the right places. They looked, in particular, structurally similar to glucagon itself — as if the gene encoded glucagon and two cousins of glucagon, all to be cut out of the same string.

The cousins did not yet have functions. They did not yet have names. Habener's group called them, for lack of anything better, glucagon-like peptide 1 and glucagon-like peptide 2. GLP-1 and GLP-2.

Nobody knew what either one did.

The Habener lab in those years had a particular character. It was a working laboratory, not a fancy one — MGH's research space in the early 1980s was functional rather than handsome — and it ran on the labor of postdoctoral fellows, graduate students, and a small permanent core of senior scientists. Two of these are relevant here.

The first is Svetlana Mojsov. Mojsov was a Macedonian-born biochemist who had come to the United States for graduate school, taken her Phd at Rockefeller University in New York under the great peptide chemist Bruce Merrifield (Nobel Prize for chemistry, 1984), and arrived at MGH in the early 1980s as a research scientist with a particular skill: she could make peptides. That is, she could synthesize, atom by atom, short proteins of defined sequence — including peptides nobody had ever made before, peptides that did not exist outside her flask. This is harder than it sounds. Peptide synthesis in the early 1980s was still as much craft as technique; the chemistry was finicky, the yields were uneven, and the difference between a working synthesis and a failed one was often a matter of small adjustments understood only by the chemist doing the work. Mojsov was very good.

The second is Daniel Drucker, who arrived at the Habener lab as a postdoctoral fellow in 1984. Drucker was Canadian, a physician by training, and had come south for a few years to work on hormone biology before going back to set up his own laboratory at the University of Toronto. He was, by all accounts, intense and prolific even then. (He has remained intense and prolific. As of this writing, in 2026, Drucker is still running his Toronto lab and is, at this point, the closest thing the GLP-1 field has to a senior statesman.) Habener, Mojsov, Drucker, a handful of others, and various transient students and technicians shared the project of figuring out what GLP-1 and GLP-2 actually did in the body. This is the kind of project that, in retrospect, sounds glamorous and was at the time mostly tedious. You have a peptide that the body apparently makes. You do not know which cells make it. You do not know which tissues it acts on. You do not know, when it gets to those tissues, what it tells them to do. You have a sequence on a piece of paper and an unstable molecule in a flask and a whole organism's worth of plausible places to look.

The first job was finding where in the body GLP-1 was made. This turned out to be, mostly, the gut — specifically, a class of cells in the small and large intestine called L cells. The same cells that, in pancreatic tissue, processed proglucagon into glucagon, processed it differently in the gut. In the pancreas, the cleavage machinery cut out glucagon and left the GLPs in the discarded packing material. In the gut, the same precursor was cut differently, releasing the GLPs as their own peptides while breaking glucagon up into smaller pieces. The same gene, in two tissues, was making different hormones.

A reader new to molecular biology may want to pause on this for a moment. It is, on first encounter, slightly miraculous. The cell decides what to make by choosing where to cut. The gene is the same. The precursor is the same. The product depends on the wielder of the knife.

The second job was figuring out what the gut peptides did. Here the trouble started.

Initial experiments using the most obvious version of GLP-1 — the version corresponding directly to the stretch of precursor sequence — produced disappointing results. The peptide did not seem to do much of anything. It bound weakly to a few cell types. It did not, in particular, seem to stimulate insulin secretion in the way an incretin should. Various groups around the world, replicating the experiment, got similarly underwhelming results. By the middle of the 1980s the peptide was looking like a structural curiosity rather than a working hormone — an evolutionary leftover, perhaps, the kind of thing a genome holds onto without really using.

This was wrong. The peptide they were testing was not the active form.

Mojsov is the one who worked this out. The active form of GLP-1 was not the full peptide encoded by the gene; it was a shorter version, missing the first six amino acids, that the body produced by an additional cleavage step. The full peptide was inactive. The trimmed peptide — GLP-1(7-37), in the field's notation, meaning amino acids seven through thirty-seven of the original — was hundreds of times more potent. Mojsov synthesized this shorter form, working out the chemistry herself, and put it into experiments. The shorter form did everything the longer form had failed to do. It bound tightly to cells. It stimulated insulin secretion strongly, and — crucially — it did so in a glucose-dependent way: only when blood sugar was elevated, not all the time. This is the magic property of an incretin. You want a hormone that pushes the pancreas to release insulin when there is sugar to deal with, and leaves the pancreas alone when there is not. Otherwise you would crash your blood sugar between meals.

This was the missing piece. The hormone was real. The hormone was an incretin. The hormone was, in fact, the second incretin the field had been looking for, alongside GIP, since the 1960s.

The papers reporting all of this came out in 1986 and 1987. Habener was the senior author on most of them. Mojsov was on the author lists — often prominently, sometimes, more controversially, less prominently than her work would have warranted. Drucker, by then deep in his postdoctoral years, contributed his own studies of how the precursor was processed differently in different tissues; he would carry the work to Toronto with him and build the largest GLP-1 program in the world out of it. Other groups — at the Hammersmith Hospital in London, at the German Diabetes Center in Düsseldorf, at the University of Copenhagen — confirmed and extended the findings. The picture sharpened.

By the end of the 1980s, GLP-1 was an established hormone. It had a sequence. It had a tissue of origin. It had a measurable function. It was an incretin, secreted by gut cells in response to a meal, that traveled through the blood to the pancreas and told it to release insulin in proportion to how much sugar was around.

It also did some other things.

It slowed gastric emptying — meaning it told the stomach to take its time releasing food into the small intestine. (Anyone who has eaten an enormous meal and felt full for hours has a rough sense of what slow gastric emptying feels like.) It seemed, in some experiments, to act on the brain — to tell the animal, somehow, that it was full. Different groups reported different results on this last point. The brain effects were intriguing and messy. Most researchers in the late 1980s noted them in their discussion sections and moved on.

A reasonable person, looking at this picture in 1989, would have noticed something. People with type 2 diabetes have a broken insulin response. Their pancreases do not release enough insulin when they eat. A natural hormone that makes the pancreas release more insulin — but only when blood sugar is high, so it cannot crash a person's blood sugar — would be, if you could deliver it to a patient, more or less an ideal diabetes drug. Better than insulin itself, in some ways: more selective, less prone to cause hypoglycemia, working with the body's own machinery rather than overriding it.

People did notice. The first trials of GLP-1 infusions in human diabetics began in the late 1980s and early 1990s, mostly in Europe. They worked. Patients with type 2 diabetes, given GLP-1 by intravenous drip, lowered their blood sugar substantially. They also reported, off-handedly, that they were less hungry. A few of them, given infusions over multiple days, lost a little weight. The investigators noted the appetite effect and mostly moved on; the trials were about glucose, not weight.

There was, however, a wall. GLP-1, as a drug, was useless. Not because it did not work, but because it disappeared from the bloodstream within minutes. The body has an enzyme — DPP-4, dipeptidyl peptidase 4 — that lives on the surface of cells throughout the body and chews up GLP-1 almost as fast as it can be produced. The natural peptide, infused into a patient, has a half-life of one to two minutes. To use it as a drug, you would need to give it as a continuous intravenous infusion, around the clock, forever. No diabetic was going to do this. Pharmaceutical companies, briefly excited by the early human data, looked at the half-life problem and went home.

This, in the late 1980s and early 1990s, was where the field sat. There was an interesting hormone. There were promising human results. There was a brick wall in the form of an enzyme called DPP-4 that chopped the hormone into pieces faster than anything could be done with it.

This is the moment, more or less, when an endocrinologist in a basement room in the Bronx — having decided, on a hunch, that lizards might be doing something interesting with glucose — was beginning to extract a peptide that turned out to have, among its other peculiarities, one peculiar property in particular. The DPP-4 enzyme could not chew it up. The lizard, evolving over tens of millions of years to live on a few meals a year, had developed a version of the hormone that the human enzyme could not see.

Eng's lizard peptide, exendin-4, looked like GLP-1 enough to bind to GLP-1's receptor. It did not look like GLP-1 enough to be cut up by DPP-4. Where natural GLP-1 lasted minutes, exendin-4 lasted hours.

The wall was about to be climbed, by a man almost no one in the field had heard of, working alone, on his own dime, in a hospital basement nobody at the relevant pharmaceutical companies could have located on a map.

For this chapter, here is what to take away. A hormone called GLP-1 was discovered, reluctantly and almost by accident, in the early 1980s, by a small set of laboratories working on something else. Its discovery was a side effect of a different question: how is glucagon made, and what else does the same gene make besides? The answer turned out to involve a hormone with metabolic effects nobody had been looking for. By the end of the 1980s, the hormone's basic function was clear, its potential as a diabetes treatment was clear, and its uselessness as a drug — given how fast the body destroyed it — was also clear. The field needed a more durable version of the molecule. The field did not yet know that one had been cooking, for the past forty million years, in the salivary glands of a desert lizard.

It will take fifteen more years from here for any of this to reach a patient. Most of those years will be spent, by various people in various countries, trying to make the durable version work as a drug, and trying to figure out — once they have a working drug — what it actually does.

But before we follow the durable version into the world, we need to understand the disease it was being built to treat. Diabetes is older than its name. It is also more various, and more confusing, and more consequential than most short summaries make it out to be. The next chapter is a detour, but a necessary one. To understand why GLP-1 mattered as a drug, we have to understand what diabetes is, and what diabetes is not, and why the field had been waiting, for half a century, for something that could do what GLP-1 promised to do.
