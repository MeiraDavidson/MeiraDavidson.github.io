The Tumor in the Backbone

from The Recipe and the Cook — A Short Book About mRNA

sid sijbrandij started having chest pains one november day in 2022. Not the sudden grab-your-heart kind. The dull, achy kind that doesn’t go away — the kind that makes you wonder if you slept on it funny, or pulled something reaching for the cereal. He was forty-three. He ran a software company called Gitlab — one of those companies most people have never heard of but that programmers all over the world use every day. Dutch by birth, living in San Francisco, married, two kids, and absolutely no reason to think anything serious was going on inside him.

After about a week of the pain not going away, he went to the emergency room. The doctors there slid him into a giant donut-shaped machine called a CT scanner — the one that spins around you and takes pictures of your insides — and they looked at what came out.

What came out was bad news.

There was a lump in Sid’s chest. Not in his lungs. Not in his heart. In one of the bones of his spine. The fifth one down from the base of his neck — you can find it on yourself if you put two fingers at the top of your back and slide them down a little. The lump was about the size of a tennis ball. Bones are not supposed to grow tennis balls. Whatever this lump was, it was growing where it shouldn’t, and it was leaning on things that needed the room.

The radiologist who saw it first called Sid’s wife from another room. By morning the doctors had a guess about what they were looking at. They called it osteosarcoma, which is a fancy word that means “cancer that grew out of bone.” It’s the kind of cancer that mostly happens to teenagers, weirdly enough — kids who are still shooting up fast. It almost never happens to grown men in their forties. Every doctor Sid talked to that week kept asking, in a very polite voice, whether he was really forty-three. Which he was.

So here is Sid. On a Monday, going to the ER for chest

pains. By Friday, a man with a tumor in his spine that shouldn’t be there, growing out of a bone, made of cells that have forgotten the rules and are dividing and dividing and dividing.

Most people, in this situation, do exactly what their doctors tell them to do.

Sid did that. But Sid also did something else, and that something else is the reason you and I are sitting here, reading this book together.

The first thing he did, after the diagnosis and the second opinions and the long flat car rides home from various medical buildings, was open up a blank document on his computer. He gave it a title — his own name — and he started typing.

Everything he had learned. Every word a doctor had said that he didn’t understand. Every paper he had Googled. Every drug name. Every side effect. Every statistic. Every protocol. He typed it all into one document, organized it, indexed it, and kept adding to it. Within a few months the document was over a hundred pages long. Within a year it would pass a thousand.

This is, on its face, a slightly weird thing to do. Most people who get a serious diagnosis don’t start a personal encyclopedia about themselves. They worry, they cry, they call their parents, they show up for the appointments. They let the doctors handle it. The doctors, after all, are the ones who know things.

But Sid was a software guy. And software guys, when

they encounter a system they don’t understand, have a particular reflex. They open a document. They start writing things down. They look for the source code. They assume that any system, no matter how complicated, is something a sufficiently patient person can eventually read and reason about. They don’t believe in magic. They believe in files.

So Sid started reading the source code of his own cancer.

And then — and this is the part that still makes oncologists raise their eyebrows when they hear about it — Sid did the next obvious thing for a person who grew up in the open-source software world. He put the document on the public internet. He bought a domain name, osteosarc.com, and he started publishing what he was learning. Other people with osteosarcoma could read it. Their families could read it. Anyone could read it. Doctors could read it and yell at him about the parts he had gotten wrong, which they sometimes did, and which he was grateful for, because then he could fix the parts he had gotten wrong.

If you go to osteosarc.com right now, today, you can still read it. You can see, in detail you might find uncomfortable, what was found inside Sid’s body in November of 2022 and every month since. You can see his scan reports. You can see his blood counts. You can see, in carefully worded sections, what his doctors disagreed with each other about and how those disagreements got resolved.

A lot of people, encountering this for the first time, find it strange. There is a long tradition in medicine — older than penicillin, older than the stethoscope — of treating a patient’s diagnosis as a private and somewhat shameful piece of information. The patient is supposed to be the one who doesn’t know things. The doctor is supposed to be the one who does. The treatment is something that happens to the patient, the way the weather happens to a sailor.

Sid wasn’t having any of that. If his cancer was a system, then his data was just data, and his data — like all good data — was more useful in the open.

This turns out to matter, eventually, in a way nobody

could have predicted at the time. But we’re getting ahead of ourselves. In November of 2022, the document was small and Sid was scared and his wife was scared and his kids didn’t fully understand what was happening. None of the things this book is about had happened yet.

The first thing the doctors had to do was take the tumor out. This is harder than it sounds.

Imagine you have a tennis ball that is somehow stuck to the central wooden beam holding up your house. You’d like to remove the tennis ball. You would not like to remove the beam. You would also like the wall not to fall down while you’re working.

Sid’s tennis ball was wedged against his spinal cord — the bundle of nerves running down the middle of his backbone, the one that lets his arms and legs work. The surgeons couldn’t just cut the tumor out, because the part of the vertebra it was growing out of was also part of the structural scaffolding holding his torso up. They had to take out the bone too. And then they had to replace it.

In January of 2023, a team of surgeons in San Francisco opened Sid up from the back, removed his fifth thoracic vertebra, removed the tumor stuck to it, and built him a new vertebra out of titanium and some bone graft material — basically a small metal Lego brick that goes where the bone used to be. They screwed it into the vertebrae above and below it with long titanium rods.

He was, after this, slightly shorter than he had been before. About two centimeters. He came home from the hospital walking carefully and not quite the same height he started at.

And that was supposed to be the end of it. The tumor was out. The bone was replaced. The margins around the tumor — the layer of healthy-looking tissue the surgeons leave around any cancer they remove — looked clean. The pathology report came back saying, yes, definitely osteosarcoma, but probably localized. The doctors started him on chemotherapy as a precaution, the standard cocktail that osteosarcoma patients have been getting for decades, and they told him the next year was going to be unpleasant but the prognosis was reasonable.

It was unpleasant. The drugs they use against osteosarcoma are the old, brute kind — chemicals from the 1970s and 1980s that work by being toxic to fast-growing cells in general. They don’t know cancer from your hair, your stomach lining, or your bone marrow. They just hit everything that’s dividing and hope the cancer dies first. Sid’s hair fell out. His mouth filled with sores. He spent days on couches and floors. He kept working at Gitlab in between rounds, in part because the company depended on him and in part because there are only so many hours a person can spend thinking about their own cells.

By summer of 2023, he had finished the chemo, and the

scans were clean. Officially he was in remission. The doctors used the word “cured” carefully, because in oncology you never really say cured, you say “no evidence of disease,” but the disposition was good. He had survived the surgery, he had survived the chemo, he was back at work, and the tennis ball was gone.

He kept updating the document.

In January of 2024, almost exactly a year after the surgery, the tumor came back.

Not in the same place. The surgeons had done their job; the titanium brick was holding, the spine was stable, and the original site was clean. The tumor came back somewhere else. The doctors found small spots — new ones, growing — in his lungs.

When a cancer comes back somewhere other than where

it started, doctors use the word “metastatic,” which comes from a Greek word that means “to change place.” The cancer has gotten on a plane. It has booked a ticket to a different organ. Some cells from the original tumor had slipped out before the surgery, traveled through his bloodstream or his lymphatic system, and put down roots somewhere new. They had probably been there since before the surgery. They had probably been there during the chemo. The chemo had not gotten them.

This is the part of cancer that nobody likes to talk about and that everybody who has cancer ends up thinking about a lot. The surgery you can see. The drugs you can see. But the cells that left before anyone knew they were leaving — those cells are out there somewhere, and there isn’t a scan in the world that can find a tumor smaller than a few millimeters.

Until they grow big enough to show up on the imaging,

they’re invisible. You don’t know if they exist. You don’t know how many there are. You don’t know where.

When the metastases showed up in Sid’s lungs, the standard playbook for osteosarcoma was: more surgery, more chemo, repeat. The lung metastases get cut out one by one, in operations called wedge resections, where the surgeons remove a small pie-slice of lung tissue containing the spot.

Then another round of the same chemo drugs. Then more

scans. Then more wedge resections if more spots appear. You play whack-a-mole until the moles stop showing up, or you run out of lung, or the cancer figures out a way to keep ahead of you.

Sid did the whack-a-mole. Several rounds of it. The document got longer.

But he was also starting to read more widely. He read

papers — not abstracts, papers — on the molecular biology of his disease. He talked to researchers all over the world. He hired a small medical research team to read alongside him. He set up calls with oncologists at half a dozen of the best cancer centers in the United States and Europe. He asked them the same questions and listened to where their answers differed.

What he was figuring out, slowly, was that the standard playbook for adult-onset osteosarcoma was not a very good playbook. Not because the doctors were doing anything wrong. The playbook was the playbook because nobody had built a better one. Osteosarcoma is a rare cancer; adult osteosarcoma is a rarer cancer; the drug companies that fund the big clinical trials don’t fund trials for diseases that affect only a few thousand people a year, because the math doesn’t work for them. So the playbook he was on was, in essence, the playbook that had existed when he was a child.

The newer ideas in cancer treatment — the ones you might have heard about, the ones that come with words like immunotherapy and targeted therapy — those were happening, but mostly for the common cancers. For breast cancer. For lung cancer. For melanoma. The places where the patient populations are big enough to support the trials.

For Sid, the menu had three things on it. Surgery. Chemotherapy. Radiation. The same three things it had had for fifty years.

This is where most patients run out of options. The protocol exists. The protocol is the protocol because nothing else has been proven to work better. If you keep going off-protocol, you’re now in territory where nobody can promise you anything, where the insurance gets weird, where you start getting on planes to consult with people in other countries, where your friends start asking your wife if you’re okay.

Sid kept going.

In the late summer of 2024, he started talking seriously with a young oncologist named Jacob Stern. Stern wasn’t from any of the big cancer centers Sid had been consulting with. He had a smaller practice, a research orientation, and an interest in something most oncologists were not yet doing for individual patients: building treatments from scratch, one patient at a time, using a relatively new and still slightly experimental technology.

What Stern was interested in, and what he and Sid started talking about, was the possibility of designing a vaccine.

Stop. I know what you’re thinking. Vaccines are for things you don’t have yet. Vaccines are how you don’t get measles. Vaccines are something you got as a baby and again in 2021, against the coronavirus. Vaccines are not how you treat a cancer that’s already inside you.

That used to be true. It is no longer entirely true. And the reason it is no longer entirely true is the central subject of this book.

The very short version, for now, is this. Vaccines work by teaching your immune system to recognize something. They show your immune system a picture of the enemy, ahead of time, so your immune system can practice. When the real enemy shows up later, your immune system already has wanted posters printed.

For most of the history of vaccines, the “enemy” you were showing your immune system was a virus or a bacterium. The polio virus. The measles virus. Smallpox. The picture you were showing your immune system was a picture of the same enemy that any other person might encounter — the same virus that gets everyone, more or less.

But what if the enemy is yours alone? What if the enemy is inside you already, and what’s more, the enemy is made out of you — your own cells, with your own particular set of mutations, going their own particular wrong direction? What if no two cancers, even of the same official name, are quite the same thing at the molecular level?

Then you would need a different kind of vaccine. You

would need a wanted poster of the specific criminals inside this specific person. You would need to know, in detail, which proteins on the surface of Sid’s tumor cells looked weird in a way that Sid’s immune system could be taught to spot.

To do that, you would need to read Sid’s tumor. Every

gene. Every mutation. You would need to compare the tumor cells to his normal cells and figure out where they had drifted. You would need to ask a computer which of those drifts were likely to be visible to the immune system. You would need to pick a few of the most promising candidates. And then you would need to manufacture a drug — a specific drug, for a specific person, made one time — that taught Sid’s immune system to look for exactly those weird proteins.

For most of medical history this would have been a fantasy. The technology to read a tumor’s genes in detail didn’t really exist until the last decade or so, and even then it was expensive. The technology to predict which mutations would be visible to which immune system has only become workable in the last few years, riding on the same wave of AI tools that you’ve seen everywhere else. And the technology to manufacture a custom drug for one specific person, in a reasonable amount of time and at a reasonable cost — that one is even newer.

The thing that makes the manufacturing possible — the

thing that makes all of this possible — is a molecule called messenger RNA. mRNA. You may have heard of it, in passing, in 2021. The COVID vaccines were made of it.

The same molecule that ended up in 13 billion vaccine

doses given to most of the world’s adults during the pandemic — the same molecule that was, for a few months in 2021, the most-injected substance in human history — turns out to be the molecule you would use if you wanted to make a different drug, one at a time, for one specific person.

We will get to why. That’s the rest of the book.

For now, what matters is that in the fall of 2024, Sid Sijbrandij decided that he was going to try to be one of the very few people in the world to have a vaccine designed and built specifically for his own cancer. He was going to read his tumor. He was going to pick targets. He was going to find a lab that could manufacture an mRNA drug to teach his immune system about those targets. He was going to inject the drug into his arm. And then he was going to find out, in real time, by counting cells in his own blood, whether his immune system was learning what he had asked it to learn.

It was going to cost something on the order of millions of dollars. Some of that he would pay for himself. Some of it would be done by labs and companies who agreed to help because Sid had been generous to them in other ways or because they were curious. He was going to do all of this outside the normal clinical trial system, under a U.S. regulatory pathway called individual-patient expanded access, which is a fancy way of saying: if a patient is out of options and a doctor wants to try something not yet approved, there is a paperwork path that makes it legal.

He would be one person. The trial would be n=1.

His document would record everything.

Here is what I want to ask you to hold in your head as we go through this book.

If you had told a thoughtful, well-informed person in 1995 that thirty years later a guy in San Francisco would get cancer, post 25 terabytes of his own genetic data on the public internet, hire an AI to read it, get a drug designed specifically for the mutations in his own tumor, and inject that drug into his own arm at a cost roughly equal to a nice house in the suburbs — that thoughtful person would have raised their eyebrows pretty far.

Now hold this in your head too: in late 2025, almost exactly while Sid was finishing his treatment, a man in Sydney with no medical background did roughly the same thing for his dog. We will get to that later, but I want it sitting in your peripheral vision the whole time we’re walking through the science. The story isn’t just that one wealthy CEO got an extraordinary treatment for himself. The story is that the gap between what is possible for an extraordinary patient with extraordinary resources and what is possible for a regular person with internet access is closing, fast, and in ways that almost nobody outside a few specialized labs is yet talking about clearly.

That’s why I wanted to start with Sid. Not because he is special, though he is in some ways. Because he was first, or among the first, to walk through a door that more and more people are now walking through. The molecule that made his treatment possible is the same molecule that made the COVID vaccines possible, and it is the same molecule that is going to make a category of medicine possible that didn’t exist a decade ago — personalized, programmable, designed in software, manufactured to order.

To understand any of that, we have to start with the molecule. We have to figure out what mRNA actually is — not what the news called it during the pandemic, but what it actually is, inside cells, doing the job it was doing for about four billion years before anyone got the idea of putting it in a syringe.

The good news is that the molecule is not very complicated. You already have, inside you, somewhere on the order of half a million different mRNA molecules right now, doing things, while you read this. They are not foreign to you. They are how you work. The trick of the mRNA vaccine is not magic. It is borrowed. The vaccine designers found a molecule that your cells already speak fluently, and they wrote a sentence in your cells’ own language, asking them to make a single protein your cells had never seen before. That’s it.

That’s the whole trick.

To understand the trick we need to spend a little while inside a cell, looking around. There is a kitchen in there. There is a cook. There are recipes coming down from a library upstairs, getting unfolded on the counter, getting read, getting followed.

That’s the next chapter.

For now, the thing to remember about Sid is this: in November of 2022 he had a tumor he didn’t know about. In January of 2023 he had a tumor removed and a piece of titanium installed. In January of 2024 the cancer came back. Through 2024 he ran out of standard moves. By the fall of 2024 he had decided to try to make a drug for himself, and he had hired people to help him do it.

And the thing he was trying to do — design a drug for

one person, in software, and have it manufactured in a few months — would not have been possible at all five years before he tried. It was made possible by exactly two things: the rapid, pandemic-driven scale-up of the mRNA vaccine industry, and the equally rapid arrival of AI tools that could read a tumor’s genome and predict which of its mutations the immune system might be able to see.

He was, in a sense, riding two waves at once. Both waves had crested in 2020 and 2021, while he was still healthy, while none of us knew his name. By the time he needed them, they were there.

The rest of this book is the story of how those waves got built — by a Hungarian biochemist nobody would fund, by a husband-and-wife team in a German lab who saw a paper from Wuhan and rearranged their entire company over breakfast, by a lipid chemist in Vancouver who spent three decades on a problem nobody else cared about — and what those waves now make possible, for people like Sid, and people like the guy in Sydney with the dog, and quite possibly, in the next decade, for people like you.

We begin with the molecule.
