February: Inside the Barn

from A Year on a Dairy Farm — Twelve Months on a Finger Lakes Dairy

It is four in the morning, the thermometer outside the kitchen window reads minus six, and Tom Hartley is putting on the second of his two pairs of socks. The first pair is thin wool, the second thick. Then Carhartt overalls over long underwear, then the boots, then a flannel, then the brown duck coat that is missing two buttons because he keeps meaning to fix them and keeps not fixing them. A wool hat. Gloves in the pocket — he won’t need them yet, inside.

The kitchen is dark except for the small light over the stove. The coffee is in a thermos; he made it the night before and reheated it just now in the microwave for forty seconds, which is exactly long enough. The radio is on low — the Geneva station, weather and farm news, a man’s voice talking about hay prices in a tone that suggests he has been talking about hay prices for thirty years and intends to keep going. Sarah is upstairs, asleep. Their daughter Anna, home from Cornell for the long weekend, is asleep in her old room. The dog, an aging border collie named Jess who has stopped being useful around cows but is still useful around the kitchen, lifts her head from the rug, considers the situation, and decides to stay where she is.

Tom goes out the back door. The cold is the kind that feels personal. It enters the inside of his nose and turns the small hairs there into a foreign material. He walks the eighty yards from the house to the barn on a path he has shoveled and re-shoveled all week, the snow piled waist-high on either side. The Finger Lakes do not get the heaviest snow in New York — that distinction belongs to the cities along Lake Ontario, Watertown and Oswego, where the lake-effect bands stack up — but they get enough, and they get the kind of cold that comes off the long fingers of water before the lakes freeze and then sits in the valleys for weeks.

The barn comes up out of the dark like a ship. It is a long low building, a free-stall barn, built by Tom’s father in 1978 and added onto twice since. The east-facing windows of the milkhouse are already lit. Marcelino has been here since a quarter to four. He is from a town in the highlands of Guatemala that Tom has looked up on a map exactly once, in 2019, when Marcelino first came, and which Tom could not now find again without looking again. Marcelino speaks Spanish to Tom and Mam to his wife on the phone, and the phone is in his shirt pocket, on speaker, more often than not, because his daughter, who is seven, likes to hear what is happening even when there is nothing to say.

Tom slides open the door to the milkhouse and the warm hits him in the face. It is not warm warm. It is forty-five degrees, maybe forty-eight, and after minus six this is the tropics. The milkhouse smells like detergent and stainless steel and faintly of milk, which is the cleanest smell in any working dairy and the one that, if you have ever lived around it, you can identify in a stranger’s coat from across a room. Marcelino is at the wash sink, hosing down a length of pipeline. He nods. Buenos días. Buenos. The vacuum pump is already running, a steady low hum that you stop hearing after about a minute and then notice again, like the refrigerator at home, only when it cuts out.

Through the next door is the parlor — a double-six herringbone, which means twelve cows can be milked at a time, six on each side, standing at an angle to the pit where Tom and Marcelino will work. The pit is a trench three feet lower than the cow platform, so the cow’s udder is at chest height for a person of average build, which Tom is and Marcelino is not; Marcelino works on a small wooden box he made himself, two winters ago, and which he has carried into the pit every morning since. The pit smells like rubber and warm metal and very faintly of the iodine teat dip in its bottles along the wall.

Beyond the parlor is the barn proper. This is where you would go if you wanted to understand what a dairy farm in February actually is.

It is two hundred feet long and seventy feet wide and the roof is high, and the cows are in it. There are ninety-four of them this morning. They are lying in stalls bedded with sawdust, or standing at the feed bunk pulling at the long ropy strands of baleage — last summer’s grass, cut and wrapped wet in white plastic and fermented into something between hay and silage — or walking, slowly, in the alley between the rows of stalls. The barn is open at the eaves; in summer the breeze moves through it; in winter the warmth of the cows themselves, ninety-four bodies of about fourteen hundred pounds each, keeps the inside of the building twenty to thirty degrees warmer than the outside. You walk in and you can feel them before you see them. You can hear them: the low rumbling of cud chewing, which is constant, and the occasional deep exhale, and the sound of a hoof on the concrete alley, and the wet pulling sound of a tongue wrapping around hay.

The smell is not what people expect. People who have never been in a well-run dairy barn expect it to smell like manure, and there is manure — there is always manure, it is the by-product of the entire operation and a dairy farm is in many ways a manuremanagement business that happens to also produce milk — but a clean barn does not smell bad. It smells like grass that has been turned into another thing. It smells, faintly, sweet. There is the musk of the cows themselves, which is its own warm animal smell, and the diesel of the skid steer parked near the door, and the ammonia note that rises when fresh manure hits old straw, but underneath all of it is the grass, fermented and broken down and passing slowly through the ninety-four enormous four-chambered stomachs that surround you in the dark.

The lights are on, but only the work lights — long fluorescent tubes down the center alley, leaving the stalls in a softer half-dark. The cows do not need the brightness, and Tom does not want to startle the ones who are still lying down. They will get up in their own time, when the parlor calls them.

He walks the length of the barn. He does this every morning and he is doing it now: a slow walk down the feed alley, looking at each cow he passes, not in any deliberate way but in the way a person looks at the rooms of his own house when he comes home at night. He is checking, without quite knowing he is checking, that everything is the same as it was. A cow lying down with her head turned around against her flank: normal, she is sleeping. A cow standing alone at the back of a stall, not eating, ears low: not normal, look again. A cow with her tail lifted: about to do one of two things, both of which Tom has seen ten thousand times.

He passes the milking string — the cows currently in production, the ones who will go into the parlor in twenty minutes — and continues to the back of the barn, to a smaller pen separated by a metal gate.

This is the dry pen. There are eleven cows in it. They are the cows who are pregnant and close to calving, who have been taken off milking for the last two months so their bodies can rest and put their energy into the calf instead of into the bucket. They are fed differently from the milking cows — less rich, more roughage — and they are watched more carefully, because this is when things go wrong, and when things go right.

In the back corner, lying down, is a Jersey-Holstein cross with a black-and-white face and a soft brown muzzle, ear tag number 47. Her name is Hazel. She is six years old. She has had five calves already and she is three weeks from her sixth. Her belly, even lying down, is enormous; you can see, if you stand still long enough, the calf inside her shifting. Tom leans on the gate and watches her for a minute. She watches him back, calmly, the way she has watched him every morning of her life.

This is where the year begins. Not in March, when the calf will come. Here, in the dark of a February barn, with a pregnant cow and a man leaning on a gate and the vacuum pump humming through the wall and a worker in the next room rinsing a pipeline and a farm and a family and a small economy of milk and grass and labor and weather all waiting, quietly, for the thing that is about to happen.

It is four-twelve in the morning. The first cows are getting up.

The first group of twelve comes in on their own. The parlor has a return lane that loops around behind the milking pit, and the cows know the route the way you know the route from your bedroom to the bathroom in the dark. They walk in, find their stanchions, lower their heads to the small pile of grain in front of each one — the only bribe the system requires — and stand still. Tom and Marcelino move down the line. A spray of pre-dip on each teat, a wipe with a clean towel, attach the milking unit, move to the next cow. The unit pulses and pulls; the milk runs through clear lines into the bulk tank in the next room, where it is chilled within seconds to thirty-eight degrees. Twelve cows take about eight minutes, start to finish. There are seventy-six cows in the milking string this morning. Six and a half rounds. They will be done by seven-thirty.

This is the compressed version. Another chapter will tell it slowly. For now: it is a rhythm, and the rhythm is the same as it was yesterday and the same as it will be tomorrow, and there is a kind of comfort in this even when your back hurts, which Tom’s does, in the same place it always hurts, just below the right shoulder blade, where he tore something thirty years ago lifting a hay bale he should not have lifted alone.

By eight he is back in the kitchen. The light is up now, the kind of February light that doesn’t really commit to being daylight — gray, low, indirect, like the sky is making a half-effort. Sarah is at the stove. Anna is at the table in pajama pants and one of Tom’s old flannel shirts, drinking coffee and looking at her laptop. Jess is under the table.

“How’s Hazel?” Sarah says.

“Fine. Big.”

“Soon?”

“Soon.”

Anna looks up. “Is it Hazel? The one I helped pull out of the mud that time?”

“That was Hazel.”

“How old is she now?”

“Six.”

Anna considers this. “She’s old.”

“She’s not old.”

“For a dairy cow she’s old.”

Tom sits down. Sarah puts a plate in front of him: two eggs, toast, bacon she got at the farmers market in Trumansburg. He picks up his fork. “She’s not old,” he says again, to no one.

Sarah laughs, the small private laugh of a wife who has been listening to a particular argument inside her husband’s head for thirty-two years.

After breakfast the day breaks open into the things that have to be done, and the things that have not yet decided whether they have to be done. The first category includes feeding the dry cows, refilling the calf hutches with fresh straw, pushing up the feed at the bunk so the cows can reach what they had pawed away from themselves overnight, and starting the skid steer to scrape the alleys. The second category is what makes a farm a farm: the unscheduled thing that happens at 9:47 in the morning when you are halfway through scraping an alley and Marcelino comes out of the heifer barn and says the waterer is frozen.

There are two waterers in the heifer barn. They are heated. They are heated with a small electric element that has, for reasons the manual does not fully explain, decided last night not to heat. Now there is a domed cap of ice over the surface of one of them, and the heifers — six of them, between four and twelve months old — are standing around it looking at it and at Tom with the particular patience of animals who have understood that a problem exists and that it is not their problem to solve.

Tom goes back to the shop for a hammer and a kettle and an extension cord. The hammer breaks the ice. The kettle, plugged into the extension cord, pours hot water on the element. The element comes back on, eventually. The whole repair takes forty minutes and accomplishes nothing that wasn’t already supposed to be happening on its own. This is a lot of farming.

At noon Marcelino goes back to the trailer. The trailer is a single-wide set up on the slope behind the barn, on cinder blocks, with the propane tank visible from the kitchen window of the main house. Tom’s father put it in for a hired man in 1991 and it has been a hired man’s home ever since, with different hired men in it. Marcelino has been in it for almost five years. The inside is clean — Marcelino is meticulous about this — and the kitchen has a small electric kettle and a pan and a bag of dried corn for tortillas and a stack of plates with a pattern of yellow flowers around the rim that he chose at the Walmart in Geneva because his daughter had picked out the same pattern, in a smaller plate, for her grandmother in Huehuetenango.

He eats lunch alone most days. Today he eats lunch alone. He puts the phone on the counter on speaker, and his wife answers, and for a while there is the small clatter of two kitchens — one in upstate New York and one in the highlands of Guatemala — and the voice of his daughter, who has just gotten home from school, asking him if it has snowed today.

Sí, mija. Mucho. Mucho mucho.

¿Te divertiste?

He laughs.

In the afternoon Tom does the things that involve paper. He does not like the things that involve paper. He has done them his entire adult life and he does not like them yet. Sarah is at the kitchen table with the laptop and a stack of milk receipts, three months of them, and she is entering numbers into a spreadsheet and frowning at the screen the way she used to frown at her students when she was a fourth-grade teacher in Penn Yan and did not believe their excuses about their homework.

“How was last month,” Tom says, getting coffee.

“It was last month.”

“That bad?”

“Not bad. Last month.”

Tom looks at the spreadsheet over her shoulder. There is a column for income and a column for expenses and a column for difference. The difference column has both kinds of numbers in it. He does not stay long enough to count which kind there are more of. He has the kind of relationship to this spreadsheet that you have to a check engine light: it is on, you know it is on, you will deal with it, you do not need to look at it right now.

“Is Anna still here for dinner?”

“She’s leaving Sunday.”

“Good.”

He goes back outside.

The afternoon milking starts at three-thirty and ends at six. It is the same as the morning except that the light is failing instead of arriving, and the cows are slightly slower, and Tom is more tired, and Marcelino does not say much, and there are no eggs at the end of it. By six-fifteen Tom is back in the barn pushing up feed for the night. By six-thirty the cows are settling.

He does the last walk-through at twenty to seven. The barn is quiet. The cows in the milking string are lying down or eating. The dry cows are at the back. He walks the alley slowly, the way he walked it that morning, looking at the ones he passes.

Hazel is standing now, at the bunk, pulling at a rope of baleage with the steady patient work of a cow who has thirteen hours of digesting ahead of her and intends to begin. Her belly hangs low. Her ears are forward. She does not turn to look at him. She is busy.

Tom stands at the gate and watches her for a minute.

Outside, the wind is moving across the snow. Inside, it is still warm. The vacuum pump is off. The fluorescent lights are off; only the small night lights at the end of each row are on, casting the barn in a low blue. The cows breathe in the half-dark. Somewhere a hoof shifts on concrete. Somewhere a cow lets out a long, slow exhale, the kind that means she is settling for the night.

In three weeks, give or take, Hazel will have a calf.

Tom closes the gate and turns out the last of the lights and walks back across the snow to the house, where Sarah is making dinner and Anna is on the phone and Jess is under the table and the radio in the kitchen is still on, very low, talking now about something other than hay.
